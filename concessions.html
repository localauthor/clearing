Concessions (Vilnius Poems)



Bus Stop, Former Soviet Union

                    Someone has to think
                        about that trashcan
                                           I know

because it is empty
                    that trashcan

 I am seeing
 for the first time in my life

 at this bus stop shelter

              stretch of ill-used byway
                     former Soviet Union

                                       waypoint
  in the circuits of so many

                   may I never see again.



In the Morning, Kitchen

Watching you grimace through a cold sip of coffee,
the sun coming in from the east, the church
I'd found shuttered behind the complex
of restaurants and retail returned
to my mind, where the workers crouched
on concrete by the ornate gates,
lamenting their smoke breaks,
pushed me off my inclination to seek
whatever might be reclaimed of the sacred
from the desacralized: pigeons in the narthex,
oak in the cloister, electric box whirring insensate,
all announcing themselves to the world as usual,
nothing left to discover and so little to say.



The Pits

My limited local vocabulary was a passport,
I decided, to underwrite my policy of passing
through every open gate. Whatever the sign said
   was someone else's business.

I'd lived in the city a year by then
but held on to my tourist's face,
eyes wide, adrift in thought,
a soft smile to better sell the
"Who me? Sorry. Wrong turn.
Only English. Nekalbu lietuviškai."
If anyone would stop me—no one ever did—
I could backpedal slowly,
 scanning the receding details.

Fronting its idea of itself
a city discloses
the fact of its withheld,
         if not / its nature.

Storage, mostly, it turns out.

Courtyards for cars, rows
of sheds in loosening shambles,
simple gardens—passthrough
paths between thoroughfares:
the unremarkable
    bones of the everyday.

The best were the cemeteries
you'd never know from the alley,
how many names, unpronounceable to me,
stood stonefaced behind a wall
etched in hope of eternity.

When I took to thinking
the barriers remaining
     were only, and all, between me
and mute dust, how alive became my impulse
     to expose the earthly,
and a knowledge accrued of the city
     you were born in, that you would never see
as it was, became the only gift
     I could consider giving, the only reason
for writing, or anything, I could fathom
     from my own pits, behind my own locked gates,
shaken loose to reveal themselves
     and their withheld once you'd arrived.



The Logics of Preservation

It has taken some time—and will take more—
to unearth the logics of preservation here
where the values and rationales
don't spell themselves out but
disappear behind the sufficiency of what is.

I went to a famous house
or, the house of someone famous,
undergoing restoration
which they always are
assuming the goal was clear—
to restore: reestablish what was

when the curator informed me
—us, but also me—how what was
 is always bound up with when.

                  To restore a house, truly,
                           would be to raze it.
                          All else, concession.
